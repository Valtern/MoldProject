**Demonstrating the Feasibility of the VTT Model for Mold Risk Evaluation Across Building Contexts**

## Slide 2 - Problem Statement
- Mold growth in buildings is driven by temperature, relative humidity, surface condition, exposure time, and dry periods.
- The topic matters because mold affects occupant health and can damage building materials.
- The case examples in this presentation are based on three PDFs: the main VTT paper, an Indonesian low-cost housing paper, and an island residential building paper.

Speaker note: Keep this slide short and use it to frame the rest of the presentation.

## Slide 3 - Why VTT
- VTT includes the main physical variables that govern mold growth.
- Updated VTT extends the model beyond wood-only conditions.
- The model also provides sensitivity classes, which makes material mapping more practical.

Speaker note: Emphasize that VTT is useful because it is structured around the same variables that matter in real buildings.

## Slide 4 - Material Evidence from the Paper
- Table 3 divides materials into four classes: very sensitive, sensitive, medium resistant, and resistant.

## Slide 5 - Limitations That Must Be Stated
- The original VTT model was limited to unpolluted spruce/pine softwood.
- The paper also warns about real-world fluctuation and logging interval effects.
- The model is deterministic, so results must be interpreted carefully.

Speaker note: This slide protects the argument from sounding overclaimed.

## Slide 6 - Use Case 1: Low-Cost Dwellings in Surakarta
- Context: 17 low-cost dwellings in Pasar Kliwon, Surakarta were surveyed using indoor temperature and humidity time series.
- Main evidence: VTT was applied to time-series data, daily RH above 80% lasted 2.2-12.3 hours, and plywood walls showed higher cumulative mold risk.
- Practical action: log data at 10- to 60-minute intervals, map materials to sensitivity classes, and prioritize ventilation or material upgrades.

Speaker note: Explain that this example shows VTT working in a real Indonesian housing context.

## Slide 7 - Use Case 2: Island Residential Buildings
- Context: island housing faces high temperature, high humidity, and saline exposure.
- Main evidence: material vulnerability increases from reinforced concrete toward wood.
- Practical recommendation: maintain indoor conditions around 26-28°C and 50%-80% RH with dehumidification in occupied zones.

Speaker note: Point out that this use case is useful for HVAC-driven mold prevention.

## Slide 8 - How to Use VTT in PBL
- Map the building materials to the VTT sensitivity classes.
- Run the model for each key element instead of treating the whole building as one block.
- Compare the results and choose interventions based on the fastest-rising risk zones.

Speaker note: This slide is the practical bridge between the paper evidence and the assignment.

## Slide 9 - Conclusion
- VTT is suitable for PBL as a mold-risk evaluation model across building contexts.
- The claim should remain careful: VTT is a strong screening tool, not an absolute prediction tool.
- Results should always be followed by field validation.

Speaker note: End with a balanced conclusion so the presentation stays academically defensible.
